The small illustrated book, Language of Flowers (1884) is meant to be read as an illustrated dictionary of meanings for common northern hemisphere flowers, reflecting the popularity of floriography in the Victorian era. To enhance the reader’s understanding of the meaning of the flowers, the illustrator Kate Greenaway (attributed as author) drew specific images that reflect on specific flowers and their meanings and included poems that relate or mention certain flowers that are listed in the main portion of the text. While the text was originally published as a children’s educational book, it would also have been useful for new debutantes as they entered society. The illustrations and poetry add a romantic flair, which would have added to the appeal for this group when they began courting.

For our edition, we seek to introduce fans of novels from the Victorian and Georgian eras to something that might educate them about the use of flowers, particularly in the Victorian era. The information we provide via annotations is what we believe the original audience would have innately understood. To do this, we provide annotations on how the flowers might have been used, information about the poets cited in the back of the book, and we also provide some background on scholars’ research pertaining to Victorian etiquette and floriography. To connect everything together, we specifically chose to annotate flowers with meanings explicitly illustrated in the original text. We hope our target audience can still enjoy shows such as Bridgerton while bringing a more realistic understanding of real life in the Victorian era to their viewing.

One major change we do make is adding in titles to the three different sections of the text. We do this for ease of reading and understanding, but we also hope to keep the romantic flair of the original by not updating the language of the poems at the end of the book. We do make minor spelling changes as noted in the text, but we do not feel this sacrifices any part of the original or our intent to educate our audience on floriography.

    3. Poetry

    DAFFODILS William Wordsworth
    I WANDERED lonely as a cloud
    That floats on high o'er vales and hills,
    When all at once I saw a crowd,
    A host of golden Daffodils;
    Beside the lake, beneath the trees,
    Fluttering and dancing in the breeze.
    Continuous as the stars that shine
    And twinkle in the milky way,
    They stretched in never-ending line
    Along the margin of a bay:
    Ten thousand saw I at a glance,
    Tossing their heads in sprightly dance.
    The waves beside them danced; but they
    Outdid the sparkling waves in glee;
    A poet could not but be gay,
    In such a jocund company;
    I gazed and gazed, but little thought
    What wealth the show to me had brought!
    For oft when on my couch I lie,
    In vacant or in pensive mood,
    They flash upon that inward eye
    Which is the bliss of solitude;
    And then my heart with pleasure fills,
    And dances with the Daffodils.

    O LOVE WILL VENTURE IN, & c. Robert Burns
    O LOVE will venture in, where it daur na weel be seen,
    O love will venture in, where wisdom ance has been;
    But I will down yon river rove, amang the wood sae green,
    And a' to pu' a posie to my ain dear May.
    The primrose I will pu', the firstling o' the year,
    And I will pu' the pink, the emblem o' my dear,
    For she's the pink o' womankind, and blooms without a peer;
    And a' to be a posie to my ain dear May.
    I'll pu' the budding rose, when Phoebus peeps in view,
    For it's like a baumy kiss o' her sweet bonnie mou;
    The hyacinth's for constancy w' its unchanging blue,
    And a' to be a posie to my ain dear May.
    The lily it is pure, and the lily it is fair.
    And in her lovely bosom I'll place the lily there;
    The daisy's for simplicity and unaffected air,
    And a' to be a posie to my ain dear May.
    The hawthorn I will pu', wi' its locks o' siller grey,
    Where, like an aged man, it stands at break o' day,
    But the songster's nest within the bush I winna tak away;
    And a' to be a posie to my ain dear May.
    The woodbine I will pu' when the e'ening star is near,
    And the diamond-drops o' dew shall be her e'en sae clear:
    The violet's for modesty which weel she fa's to wear,
    And a' to be a posie to my ain dear May.
    I'll tie the posie round w' the silken band o' luve,
    And I'll place it in her breast, and I'll swear by a' above,
    That to my latest draught o' life the band shall ne'er remove.
    And this will be a posie to my ain dear May.

    CONSTANCY Samuel Flecther
    LAY a garland on my hearse
    Of the dismal yew;
    Maidens willow branches bear;
    Say, I died true.
    My love was false, but I was firm
    From my hour of birth.
    Upon my buried body lie
    Lightly, gentle earth!
    SAMUEL FLECTHER.
    Elegy on Captain Matthew Henderson Burns
    MOURN, ilka grove the cushat kens!
    Ye haz'lly shaws and briery dens!
    Ye burnies, wimplin down your glens,
    Wi' toddlin din,
    Or foaming strang, wi' hasty stens,
    Frae lin to lin.
    Mourn little harebells o'er the lee;
    Ye stately foxgloves fair to see;
    Ye woodbines hanging bonnilie,
    In scented bow'rs;
    Ye roses on your thorny tree,
    The first o' flow'rs.
    At dawn, when ev'ry grassy blade
    Droops with a diamond at his head,
    At ev'n, when beans their fragrance shed,
    I' th' rustling gale,
    Ye maukins whiddin thro' the glade,
    Come join my wail.
    Mourn, spring, thou darling of the year;
    Ilk cowslip cup shall kep a tear;
    Thou, simmer, while each corny spear
    Shoots up its head,
    Thy gay, green, flow'ry tresses shear,
    For him that's dead!
    Thou, autumn, wi' thy yellow hair,
    In grief thy sallow mantle tear!
    Thou, winter, hurling thro' the air
    The roaring blast,
    Wide o'er the naked world declare
    The worth we've lost!

    ARRANGEMENT OF A BOUQUET Nicholas Drayton
    HERE damask Roses, white and red,
    Out of my lap first take I,
    Which still shall run along the thread,
    My chiefest flower this make I.
    Amongst these Roses in a row,
    Next place I Pinks in plenty,
    These double Pansies then for show;
    And will not this be dainty?
    The pretty Pansy then I'll tie,
    Like stones some chain inchasing;
    And next to them, their near ally,
    The purple Violet placing.
    The curious choice clove July flower,
    Whose kind hight the Carnation,
    or sweetnest of most sovereign power,
    Shall help my wreath to fashion;
    Whose sundry colours of one kind,
    First from one root derived,
    Them in their several suits I'll bind:
    My garland so contrived.
    A course of Cowslips then I'll stick,
    And here and there (though sparely)
    The pleasant Primrose down I'll prick,
    Like pearls that will show rarely;
    Then with these Marigolds I'll make
    My garland somewhat swelling,
    These Honeysuckles then I'll take,
    Whose sweets shall help their smelling.
    The Lily and the Fleur-de-lis,
    For colour much contending;
    For that I them do only prize,
    They are but poor in scenting.
    The Daffodil most dainty is,
    To match with these in meetness;
    The Columbine compared to this,
    All much alike for sweetness.
    These in their natures only are
    Fit to emboss the border.
    Therefore I'll take especial care
    To place them in their order:
    Sweet-williams, Campions, Sops-in-wine,
    One by another neatly;
    Thus have I made this wreath of mine,
    And finished it featly.
    NICHOLAS DRAYTON.

The michaelmas daisy, for example, means "farewell" and also "afterthought" according to Kate Greenaway, but the garden daisy means "I partake of your sentiments," and the wild daisy means "I will think of it." For daisies that don't fall under these categories, Greenaway claims that the standard daisy represents "innocence" (Language). While Burns does not use this exact phrase, he does think that the daisy represents "simplicity" and an "unaffected air." So many varieties of daisies exist because people have been selectively breeding them for centuries, and now many of them can be found in the wild (Wilkinson 21). Most daisies are perennial, which means they will bloom live for three years or more, but a couple of kinds, including the michelmas daisy, are merely annual plants. They tend to bloom in early spring, and they cannot be planted too close together if they are to thrive (22). 3Is it any surprise that a book written to children and young women includes the flower associated with witchcraft and sorcery? The flower was often used medicinally, especially in its native Scotland and Britain. Some have also used it as an aprhodisiac, although this is no longer recommended (Woodland). 4Pink and purple larkspur each have their own associated meanings (fickleness and haughtiness, repsectively), and all other larkspur is associated with lightness and levity (Greenaway). This flower is not referenced in any of the poems included in this edition, but their tall stems and long stalks of flowers add interest to any bouquet. Larkspur is the annual flower in the delphinium family (Wilkinson 67), and although they are beautiful, they are poisonous to both people and animals ("Larkspur"). 5The yellow lily represents falsehood and gaiety (Greenaway), but Burns remarks that the lily is "pure" and "fair" in "The Posie." The mourning bride in particular is usually a dark red and black (Wilkinson 294). These are annual flowers that need a lot of sun (297), so they would not always grow well in England, but they do very well in southern Europe (297). With care, however, they can be grown almost anywhere. 7White mulberry trees represent wisdom, and black mulberry trees represent the feeling that without another person, one will not survive (Greenaway). They are native to the Middle East, Southeast Asia, and North America, and they are valued for the sweet fruit they produce ("Mulberry"). They can also be used as homes for silkworms ("Mulberry"). 8Periwinkle, like larkspur and daisies, has different associated meanings depending on the color of the plant. Blue periwinkle represents early friendship, and white periwinkle represents the pleasures of memory (Greenaway). Periwinkle grows as a trailing plant (Wilkinson 357), so it can create beautiful overhanging notes in any bouquet. These are native to Great Britain and do best in cool, moist climates (357). 9A posy is another word for a nosegay or a small bouquet. This was the most popular form of floral communication in Victorian England ("Nosegay"). Women would often carry posies as part of their accoutrement, and, of course, nosegays were extremely popular forms of communication from an admirer to his would-be beau. In his poem, Robert Burns is telling his readers the work he endures to show his "dear Mae" how much he loves her ("The Posie") through floral communication. Women could respond in kind, with flowers that showed their love, such as the daisy, or that showed their indifference, such as the mustard seed ("Nosegay"). 10The rose, of course, is the symbol of romantic love. Bridal roses represent happy love, and yellow roses represent jealousy. White rosebuds represent girlhood, and sending someone campions means that only they deserve the sender's love. Damask roses are a sign of freshness, and a garland of roses was a tribute to the receiver's virtue. There are over three hundred species of roses, and most of them are somehow related to love in significance and meaning. The rose that most U.S. and European readers think of when they think of a "rose" is the long-stemmed hybrid tea rose (Wilkinson 276). These are the close petals and small thorns found on the bouquets of red roses sold in most stores around Valentine's Day. North America and Great Britian do have some native species, but the classic roses that most people assocaite with love were imported from China or other Asian countries and then hybridized and cultivated into the many species we have today (273). The damask and attar roses are especially useful in creating perfumes and other scents. Growing and caring for roses is not that difficult, but the gardener must take care to keep them sheltered from the wind and provide well-drained, rich soil for their nutrients (278). 11Sweet williams are associated with gallantry, so it makes sense that they would be included in a bouquet meant to encourage a woman to accept a man's admiration, respect, and love. They do not bloom until their second year of growth, but the beauty and edibility of the flowers made it a popular garden flower in the Victorian age (70). 12Thrift is associated with sympathy. The colors of the flowers can vary in any shade of white, yellow, blue, lilac, or red, and the perennial nature guarantees blooming for at least three years (Wilkinson 312).     Note:

    Floriography was a popular study in the Victorian age, in part due to the Victorians’ obsession with reference manuals, classification, and dictionaries (Engelhardt 137). In this period, however, floriography moved from a pastime for just women to a wider project of social service and science by a growing field of amateur botanists. This study became assumed common knowledge, as Engelhardt notes that “Victorian writers expected a certain degree of floral literacy from their readers and used floral codes strategically in their fiction as subtexts for practitioners of the language of flowers” (136). These readers and writers would pull on information put out by amateur botanists in this fledgling scientific field through their reference manuals codifying flowers and their meanings so that both writers and readers could “extend the parameters of courtship and imaginative play” (156).

    This knowledge, along with the proliferation of other Victorian manuals on etiquette and other subjects, reflects a growing middle class and the emergence of new forms of marketing towards that middle class. These etiquette guides, while serving other social purposes, also show new desires and demands for commodities and material goods in the Victorian age (Weller).

    From this information, we can conclude that this booklet was one kind of informational booklet that would have been purchased for children, debutantes, and anyone else who was interested in the study of floriography, even if they were not part of the upper echelons of London society during this time.
